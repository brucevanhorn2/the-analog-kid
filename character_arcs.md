# The Analog Kid — Character Arcs
## All Six Player Characters Across Five Time Periods

---

# REVEREND THOMAS CARVER (Pastor)
**North side. First Baptist Church. Age 38 in 1955.**

---

## 1955 — "The Man With All The Answers"

Carver is 38. First Baptist is full every Sunday. He's comfortable, respected, the kind of pastor who gets invited to the mayor's office and knows it means something. His faith is institutional — he believes in God the way he believes in the American flag. Sincerely but without examination.

His 1955 problem: Corporal Eddie Briggs, Korean War veteran, sits in the back pew every Sunday smelling of bourbon and saying nothing. Eddie's wife Ruth comes to Carver. Eddie hasn't spoken a real sentence in eight months. He hit her once. She's not sure he knew he was doing it.

Carver's instinct: counsel Eddie privately, keep it inside the congregation, protect the family's dignity.

**Conversation thread:**
- Ruth at the post office — holding herself carefully, brief exchange
- VFW Hall — Eddie is there. Conversation about something else entirely. Player chooses whether to ask about Ruth, mention Carver, or let it go.
- Samuel Beaumont's practice — Samuel has seen this before. Knows what shell shock looks like medically. Can be connected to Eddie if the player builds the bridge.
- Barbershop — north side men talking. Player gauges the congregation's mood.
- Carver at First Baptist — asks the player what they think he should do. Options available depend on what the player has learned.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Counsel Eddie privately. Keep it in the congregation. Authority preserved, outcome uncertain.
- **Progressive:** Go public. Advocate for veteran mental health services. Makes enemies at the VFW.
- **Middle:** Connect Eddie to Samuel Beaumont privately. Carver and Samuel's first real connection — across the tracks, away from anyone watching. Requires the player to have visited both men.

---

## 1967 — "The Man Whose Answers Stop Working"

Carver is 50. His congregation has fractured quietly. Half his deacons think Chambers is a troublemaker. Half think Carver is a coward. Vietnam is taking boys from both sides of his congregation, but not equally. He knows about Marcus. He knows about the factory owner's son with the deferment. He said nothing in 1955. The habit of silence is now load-bearing.

His personal crisis: Bobby Simmons, 19, comes to Carver before his draft physical. He asks if it's a sin to be afraid. Carver gives him the sermon answer. Bobby ships out.

**Conversation thread:**
- Bobby before his physical — the player can push Carver toward a real answer instead of a sermon answer. Won't keep Bobby home. Might be the last honest conversation Bobby ever has.
- If Carver connected with Samuel in 1955: Samuel quietly finds a legitimate medical deferment. Bobby stays. Carver carries what he knows about how that happened.
- If Carver handled Eddie in-house: No bridge to Samuel. Bobby ships out with a scripture verse.
- Chambers organizing a march to city hall about draft inequity — asks Carver to stand beside him.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Decline. Wartime unity. Watches from his window as Chambers walks past First Baptist.
- **Progressive:** Stands with Chambers. Loses a third of his congregation by Sunday.
- **Middle:** Doesn't march but opens First Baptist as a meeting place afterward. Technically neutral. Everyone knows it isn't.

---

## 1979 — "The Man Learning New Questions"

Carver is 62. Eddie Briggs died in 1971 — heart, they said. Ruth is one of his most faithful congregants now. Bobby Simmons came back from Vietnam. He sits in the back pew where Eddie used to sit.

The Blanton accident: two of the dead are from Carver's congregation. He buries people on both sides of the tracks in the same week.

His personal crisis: Harold Blanton Jr. comes to Carver privately at 11pm. He says he isn't asking for absolution. But he's been crying.

**Conversation thread:**
- Both gravesides — north and south. Player sees Carver in each setting. Different faces in the crowd.
- Harold at 11pm — a man in genuine anguish sitting across from the only person he trusted with it. Three people are dead. Harold knew about the violations.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Counsel Harold toward quiet accountability — compensation to families, private resolution. Keeps the confession between them.
- **Progressive:** Tells Harold to go to the authorities. Harold leaves. They never speak again. Carver testifies at the inquest.
- **Middle:** Gives Harold 48 hours to come forward himself. Sits with him in silence for an hour first.

By 1979 Carver's faith has changed. Less certain. More real. The institutional comfort is gone. What's left is rawer and stronger and harder to explain on a Sunday morning.

---

## 1991 — "The Man With New Answers"

Carver is 74. Semi-retired. A younger pastor handles most Sundays. But Carver is still there.

Isaiah Chambers is dying. Cancer. They've been genuine friends since sometime in the late 1970s. Nobody made a ceremony of it. One day they were adversaries and then they weren't. Carver spends 1991 watching Chambers die and thinking about what their friendship cost both of them by coming so late.

The casino question divides his congregation. Carver has no clean answer and he's old enough to say so from the pulpit, which is new.

**Conversation thread:**
- Chambers' deathbed — Carver visits. What they say to each other depends on 36 years of accumulated choices.
- Diane Chambers (Isaiah's granddaughter, studying law) has been offered a position with the tribal council's legal team. Her parents disapprove. She comes to Carver because he knew her grandfather.
- What Carver tells Diane depends entirely on his arc:
  - If he stood with Chambers in 1967: He tells her Isaiah would have been proud. She takes the job.
  - If he watched from the window: She asks him directly what he did in 1967. The hardest conversation in his arc.

**Chambers' funeral:** Carver delivers the eulogy. Every choice since 1955 is audible in how he talks about his friend.

**The nudge (casino question):**
- **Conservative:** Counsels congregation toward caution. Gambling's social costs. Tradition.
- **Progressive:** Supports the tribal deal publicly. The justice argument. Loses conservative deacons.
- **Middle:** No public position. Opens the church for community listening sessions. Both sides. Neither comfortable.

---

## 2008 — "The Man Who Outlasted His Answers"

Carver is 91. First Baptist has changed around him. Younger congregation, different music. His associate pastor is a young man who grew up in the congregation — someone whose arc the player nudged years earlier. Carver initially resisted him. Now considers him the best thing that happened to the church in thirty years.

He's the last person in Middletown who remembers the town square in 1945 when the soldiers came home. He's become a living archive.

The financial crisis hits his congregation hard. The north side safety net — always informal, always church-based — is stretched past breaking. Carver is too old to rebuild it. He can only witness.

**Arc endpoints:**
- **Conservative path:** Respected but diminished. First Baptist is smaller. He did what was comfortable and the town changed around him without him. Not bitter. Honest about it.
- **Progressive path:** Controversial and vital. First Baptist is a community institution — food bank, meeting space, a coalition built by standing with Chambers in 1967. He spent his credibility and got something real.
- **Middle path:** Complicated and at peace. He did some things right and some things late and knows the difference. He sits in the front pew on Sundays and listens to his associate pastor deliver the sermons he should have given in 1967.

**Final scene:**
He's in his office late. Two photographs on his desk — congregation circa 1955, everyone in Sunday best. Isaiah Chambers in the 1980s, both of them laughing at something off-camera.

He doesn't explain the photographs.

*"I've been thinking about what I got wrong. There's less of it than I feared and more of it than I'd like."*

---

# DR. GERALDINE "GERI" HABICHT (Professor)
**North side. Middletown Community College. Age 35 in 1955.**
*Named after Bruce's real great-aunt Geraldine Habicht, immortalized here.*

---

## 1955 — "The Woman Who Already Won A War"

Geri is 35. PhD in Statistics from a university that didn't particularly want to give it to her. WAVES Lieutenant, Navy, WWII — calculated torpedo firing solutions under secret clearance while her German last name made neighbors uncomfortable. Demobilized in 1945. The college was what was hiring.

She teaches sociology through statistics. Her current research maps tuberculosis mortality rates by address — north vs south side. The numbers tell stories the Dean would prefer weren't told. Agnes Pruitt holds the data files in the library under a misleading catalog entry.

Lucky Strikes on her desk. An ashtray that needs emptying. She offers you one with the same clinical detachment she offers everything.

Her 1955 problem: The Dean has formally requested she transition to directing the new Nursing Education program. The letter uses the word "better suited" twice. She hasn't responded.

**Conversation thread:**
- Agnes points the player toward the science hall obliquely — doesn't betray confidence, just points
- Geri's office: first meeting. She measures the player. Presents tuberculosis data clinically. No editorializing. Watches the player's face.
- The Dean's office — player can overhear or be present when the nursing reassignment comes up formally
- Barbershop — north side men discussing "the lady professor stirring things up"
- Hospital nurse — trained under the nursing program Geri is supposed to run. Good at her job. No idea she's part of a political maneuver.
- Geri asks the player: what's the probability that publishing before responding changes the outcome versus responding first. She's done the math. She wants to know if someone else sees what she sees.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Respond to the Dean first, negotiate terms, publish later through safer channels.
- **Progressive:** Publish immediately. Force the college's hand publicly. Accept consequences.
- **Middle:** Submit research to the state health department instead — routes around the Dean entirely, harder to suppress. Requires Agnes to help with the submission quietly.

**Smoking note:** Lucky Strikes throughout. Part of the furniture of her mind.

---

## 1967 — "The Woman Whose Numbers Are Now Evidence"

Geri is 47. The tuberculosis research has aged into vindication. She has twelve more years of data. The Civil Rights Act has created institutional structures that didn't exist before.

She still smokes Lucky Strikes. The Surgeon General's report (1964) is annotated in her margins — three legitimate methodological critiques in her precise handwriting. She was still smoking while she wrote them.

Her 1967 problem: The college has been asked to formally integrate. On paper it already is. In practice, south side students in Geri's statistics courses face what she faced in 1943. She has numbers on this too. Enrollment by address. Completion rates by address. She's prepared a seventeen-page report for the new Dean with a one-paragraph conclusion and a specific policy recommendation.

She wrote the conclusion last. It took forty-five minutes. The data took twelve years.

**Conversation thread:**
- Diane Chambers (Isaiah's daughter, pre-law) — exceptional student, visibly isolated. Geri watches with clinical remove. Player can see the remove and what's underneath it.
- Agnes has been quietly ordering materials Diane needs that the south side library branch doesn't carry. Never discussed explicitly.
- Samuel Beaumont's practice — his health outcome data and Geri's enrollment data are two halves of the same picture. They've never met formally.
- VFW — player gauges how north side reads Geri's report before it's delivered.
- Geri asks player to read the report before she delivers it. Not for feedback. She wants to know what a person *feels* reading it. She's been staring at the numbers so long she can't tell if the conclusion is obvious or devastating.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Soften the policy recommendation — findings only, let administration draw conclusions.
- **Progressive:** Deliver as written. Request formal response within thirty days. Copy state board of education.
- **Middle:** Deliver the data to Samuel Beaumont first. Present jointly — statistician and doctor, harder to dismiss than either alone. Requires the Geri/Samuel connection to exist.

**Personal undercurrent:** 1967 is the year Geri stops pretending the patience is free. Not publicly. But a student catches her staring out the south-facing window at nothing. She says:

*"I've been doing the math on patience. The return on investment declines significantly over time."*

---

## 1979 — "The Woman The Town Finally Reads"

Geri is 59. Her research is cited in three state policy documents. One didn't credit her — "community health studies" in a footnote. She noted this with the same expression she uses for everything.

She's switched to filtered cigarettes — Kent or Viceroy. She hates them. She tells anyone who notices that the filtration data shows a statistically meaningful reduction in tar delivery. She says this while visibly enjoying them less.

The Blanton accident: three dead. She has the safety inspection records — public documents she pulled from county files two years ago after a statistical spike in worker injury claims nobody else noticed. She has been sitting on a predictive analysis for eighteen months.

She knew something was going to happen. She didn't know when or how bad. She calculated the probability, determined it was significant but not certain, made no formal report because she had no institutional standing and knew it would be dismissed.

She was right about the probability. She was right about the dismissal. Three people are dead.

**Conversation thread:**
- Frank DeLuca comes to the college asking general questions about industrial safety statistics. Fishing.
- Geri and Frank circle each other. Neither shows cards first.
- Harold Blanton Jr.'s daughter is in Geri's course. Earnest, not exceptional. No idea what her father did.
- Agnes has a letter from the state health department — belated formal recognition of the 1955 TB research. Arrived this week. The timing is grotesque.
- Frank asks Geri directly if she has anything relevant to his investigation.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Shares injury data but not the predictive analysis. Evidence without exposing prior knowledge. Self-protective. Not dishonest exactly.
- **Progressive:** Gives Frank everything including the analysis and a written statement about why she didn't report it. Full exposure.
- **Middle:** Gives Frank the analysis privately. Asks him to determine how it enters the investigation. Trusts his judgment. Requires the Geri/Frank relationship to have been built since 1955.

**After Frank leaves, if player stays:**

She stubs out a filtered cigarette half-finished. Looks at the ashtray.

*"I've spent thirty years telling this town what the numbers mean. I am aware of the particular irony of my own dataset."*

She picks up her pen. Goes back to work.

---

## 1991 — "The Woman Who Became The Evidence"

Geri is 71. She should have retired. She hasn't. The college tried to give her an emeritus title and a smaller office. She kept the office and ignored the title.

She's on Nicorette gum now. Chews it with the same grim determination she brought to torpedo calculations. Tried the patch — found the adhesive irritating and the dosing curve insufficiently responsive. Agnes finds gum wrappers everywhere and says nothing. It is the kindest thing she ever does for Geri.

She has forty years of Middletown data. Health outcomes, economic indicators, educational attainment by address, arrest rates by address. The north/south correlation documented across four decades. Nobody else in the world has this dataset. It lives in her office, Agnes's library, and Geri's head.

Both sides of the casino debate want her data. The tribal council's lawyers want the health disparity numbers. The conservative faction wants the economic stability numbers. Everyone wants the part that supports their position.

A journalist from a state newspaper wants to do a feature. He's smart, young, and has already decided what story he wants to tell. He wants the racial injustice angle. It's not wrong. It's also not complete.

Agnes is retiring. Her last act: pulling every document she ever misfiled for Geri and presenting them in a proper archive box. Forty years of protected research. The weight of it is physical.

**The portal sequence:**
After the player returns from Normaltown, Geri is at the corner table in Earl's Diner. Folder open. Coffee for two.

She's been running numbers on the player since 1979. She has a file. She slides it across. Inside: actuarial tables. Life expectancy calculations. A statistical impossibility documented across forty years.

*"You went through, didn't you."* Not a question.

*"I've been doing the math on you since 1979. There's only one model that fits the data."*

She closes the folder.

*"I'm not frightened. I'm curious. There's a difference."*

**The nudge (journalist question):**
- **Conservative:** Decline the interview. The data belongs to Middletown, not to a narrative.
- **Progressive:** Give him everything. Accept that incomplete truth is better than silence.
- **Middle:** Give him the complete dataset with one condition — publish the full methodology or nothing. Most journalists walk away. This one doesn't.

---

## 2008 — "The Woman Who Outlived The Question"

Geri is 88. The science hall has a hallway named after her that she finds mildly embarrassing. The library wing named after Agnes opened in 2003. Geri gave a four-minute dedication speech containing no adjectives. Agnes said it was the best speech she'd ever heard.

Agnes died in 2005. Geri kept working.

She no longer smokes. Stopped in 2001 — the day the doctor told her the damage was done but stopping would buy time. She applied the same rigor to quitting she applied to everything else.

On her desk: one Lucky Strike in a small glass case. Not framed dramatically. Just sitting there. A data point she kept.

The financial crisis threatens the sociology department — the one she built from data and stubbornness over fifty years. A younger colleague comes to her desperate. She pulls out a folder. The data on what happens to community health outcomes when sociology programs are defunded. She compiled it in 1987 because she anticipated exactly this moment. Hands it to her colleague without comment.

**Arc endpoints:**
- **Conservative path:** Data exists in official records but was never fully embraced. Department cut in 2008. Her research lives in state archives, cited occasionally, never transformative. At peace with having been right.
- **Progressive path:** Her research became institutional infrastructure — health department, tribal hospital, school integration policy, all traces back to her numbers. Never got a headline. Got outcomes. Considers this the correct exchange.
- **Middle path:** Her legacy is contested and alive. People still arguing about what her data means. She finds this deeply satisfying.

**Final scene:**
She's in her office. South-facing window. One index card on her desk. Her own handwriting. A number.

The player recognizes it. It's the index card she gave them — returned from Normaltown.

She doesn't explain how she knows.

*"I calculated the probability that any of this was real."*

Beat.

*"The math was inconclusive."*

She looks up.

*"I've decided that's sufficient."*

---

# RAYMOND "RAY" COLDWATER (Social Worker)
**South side. Tribal Community Office. Age 32 in 1955.**

---

## 1955 — "The Man Who Came Back"

Ray is 32. Grew up on the south side. Father died young, tribal council member. Mother cleaned houses on the north side six days a week. A teacher noticed him. Scholarship to a state university nobody from the south side had attended before.

He came back. That's the thing people on both sides can't understand.

He works out of the tribal community office. Official title: county social worker. What he actually does: stand between his people and every system designed without them in mind.

His practiced neutral expression — the controlled quiet of someone who learned early that a wrong reaction costs more than a right one.

His 1955 problem: The Whitehorse family. Father drinking, mother holding it together, three kids. The state child welfare office has opened a file. Ray knows what a file means.

**Conversation thread:**
- IHS clinic — Ray reviewing case files. Doesn't look up immediately.
- Whitehorse house — mother holding everything with both hands. Father not home. Oldest kid (Thomas, age 10) answers the door with the eyes of someone twice his age.
- Samuel Beaumont's practice — something medical underneath the father's drinking that hasn't been diagnosed.
- Tribal community office — Ray staring at the welfare file when player returns.
- Frank DeLuca has been to the south side twice this week. Ray noticed. Frank noticed Ray noticed.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Official channels. By the book. Slow, uncertain, exposes the family to more scrutiny.
- **Progressive:** Tribal council favor through an elder's relationship with the county commissioner. Faster. Costs the elder something Ray can't fully account for yet.
- **Middle:** Samuel Beaumont documents the undiagnosed condition, reframes the father's drinking as symptom not cause. Requires the Ray/Samuel connection. Changes the state's case entirely — but Ray has shown the welfare office he has a doctor in his corner, which changes how they see him going forward.

---

## 1967 — "The Man Holding Two Worlds Together"

Ray is 44. He's been at this twelve years. He knows which county officials can be reasoned with and which ones need to be outmaneuvered. Progress in Middletown moves in two speeds: glacial and invisible.

He carries a folded list in his breast pocket: every draft notice sent to a south side address since 1965, next to every deferment that went to a north side address. He hasn't decided what to do with it yet.

Thomas Whitehorse — the oldest Whitehorse kid, now 22 — got his draft notice. He fixes cars at a garage on Main Street. He's saving money. He hasn't told his mother yet.

**Conversation thread:**
- Tribal community office — Ray folds the list away when player arrives. Not quite fast enough.
- The garage — Thomas is still working. Shows up every day. Quiet in the same way Ray is quiet.
- Chambers' church — a meeting about draft inequity. Ray is there but not speaking. Watching who else is there.
- Frank — they've been circling each other twelve years. Frank has approximately the same list. He came to it differently.
- Geri — if connected in 1955, she has the statistical version of Ray's list. Hasn't published it. Waiting for someone to know what to do with it.

**Personal undercurrent:** Ray didn't serve in Korea. Student deferment. He's never said this to anyone. It sits in him at a particular angle.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Get Thomas a quiet deferment. Protect one person. The list stays in his pocket.
- **Progressive:** Take the list public — give it to Chambers for the march, get Geri's data alongside it. Thomas becomes a symbol he didn't sign up to be.
- **Middle:** Give the list to Frank privately. Let law enforcement find if there's a legal case. Thomas gets no promises. But the list is no longer just Ray's problem. Requires twelve years of careful relationship-building with Frank.

Thomas Whitehorse goes wherever this path leads. Ray carries the outcome.

---

## 1979 — "The Man The System Started Listening To"

Ray is 56. Tired in a way that has become structural. The tribal council has changed — younger members, more aggressive, less interested in Ray's careful navigation of white institutions.

The Blanton accident: two of the three dead are south side men. Their families come to Ray first. He knows how these things end without intervention.

He also knows something nobody else knows: one of the dead — George Runningwater, tribal member — filed a verbal complaint about the safety violations six months ago. To his foreman. Nothing written. The foreman is still employed. The complaint doesn't officially exist.

**Conversation thread:**
- IHS clinic — George Runningwater's wife. Not sick. Just somewhere to sit. She has his toolbox. Doesn't know why she brought it.
- Frank's investigation — Ray and Frank circling again. Frank is closer to the truth than Ray expected. Ray recalibrates.
- Geri — if their connection holds, she has the injury report data. Ray has the oral complaint. Together: a case. Separately: two dismissible things.
- Tribal council — younger members want to file civil suit immediately. The elder Ray trusts is dying. New voices louder.
- Thomas Whitehorse came back from Vietnam. Works at Blanton now. Standing outside the factory gates the morning after, not sure if he still has a job.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Keep the verbal complaint quiet — legally useless, surfacing it exposes the widow to a grinding process.
- **Progressive:** Surface it publicly. Shift the burden. Accept that the widow becomes the story.
- **Middle:** Give it to Frank as background, not evidence. Let Frank find a way to make it legally relevant without exposing the widow. Requires Frank to be someone Ray trusts by 1979.

Ray holds the Runningwater toolbox when the widow hands it to him for longer than necessary.

---

## 1991 — "The Man Whose People Have Power Now"

Ray is 68. The casino is coming. The tribal council now has lawyers, a PR firm, and revenue projections that make every institution in Middletown sit up straight.

Ray should feel vindicated. He mostly feels complicated.

He knows which council members are genuine and which ones have been waiting for this power and what they'll do with it. He knows the desegregation condition is brilliant and genuine simultaneously. He knows what follows casino money. He's seen Frank's crime scene photographs.

His 1991 problem: His godson (late twenties, smart) has been offered a job by the casino's management company. Real job, good money, legitimate on the surface. The management company is quietly connected to organized crime interests that followed the casino money north. Ray knows. His godson doesn't.

**The Frank and Ray scene — the best scene in the game:**

Frank brings Ray to see the crime scene photographs. Shows him without editorializing.

Frank, looking out the window at whatever Middletown has become:

*"Ray. I been a cop in this town for thirty-six years. You know what I learned? Power doesn't redeem people. It just gives them new ways to disappoint you."*

Beat.

*"I'm not talking about your people. I'm talking about all of them. Mine included."*

This is the moment Ray decides whether to trust Frank.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Tell the godson privately, strongly. Let him choose. Protect the information.
- **Progressive:** Take it to the tribal council formally. Force the connection into the open. The godson finds out Ray knew in the most public way.
- **Middle:** Give the godson enough information to ask the right questions himself. Not the conclusion — the thread. Let him pull it. Requires the player to have built both relationships carefully.

Ray is 68. He's been the bridge between worlds for thirty-six years. The bridge is still standing. He's not sure what it's connecting anymore.

---

## 2008 — "The Man Who Stayed"

Ray is 85. Should have retired fifteen years ago. Didn't, for the same reason he came back in 1955.

The tribal hospital is state of the art. His godson — who made his own choice in 1991 — is on the tribal council now. The financial crisis hits the south side differently. The casino revenue creates a buffer the north side doesn't have. For the first time in Ray's life, the south side has more institutional stability than the north.

He's not sure what to do with that. Neither is anyone else.

**Arc endpoints:**
- **Conservative path:** Careful navigation preserved relationships but not equity. Still structurally unjust. He made the peace he could make.
- **Progressive path:** Willingness to force confrontation created real institutional change. Burned relationships doing it. The ones that survived are the ones worth keeping.
- **Middle path:** Ray is the reason Middletown is still having the argument instead of having settled it wrong. Not a monument. A function. He finds this sufficient.

**Final scene:**
He's at the railroad crossing. The signal box where Marcus used to sit.

The player approaches. Ray doesn't turn around.

*"I used to watch Marcus pull that lever. Both sides of town stopping for one kid from the south side. He knew what he was doing."*

He turns around.

*"I've been trying to figure out what I was doing ever since."*

Beat.

*"I think I was building something. I'm still not sure what to call it."*

He looks at the player. Same measuring expression from 1955. Fifty years later.

*"You're still here too."*

Not an accusation. Not a question. Just an observation from a man who has been paying attention.

---

# FRANK DELUCA (Police Detective)
**North side. Middletown Police Department. Age 40 in 1955.**

---

## 1955 — "The Man Who Believes In The Law"

Frank is 40. Twenty years on the force — started as a beat cop in 1935, made detective in 1948. He grew up in Middletown, north side, son of a machinist who worked at what was then the munitions plant. He knows every street, every family, every history. He's the institutional memory of Middletown's police department and he knows it.

He believes in the law. Not the police — the law. There's a distinction he makes privately that he's never had to articulate publicly because the two haven't seriously conflicted. Yet.

His 1955 problem: A south side woman — Clara Mae Johnson, Black, 34 — was assaulted two weeks ago. She named her attacker. Frank knows the attacker. The attacker's family has money and a relationship with the police chief. The chief told Frank to let it rest.

Frank has not let it rest. He hasn't done anything yet. But he hasn't let it rest.

**Conversation thread:**
- Police station — Frank's desk has the Clara Mae file open when the player arrives. He closes it but not before the player can see the name.
- The barbershop — Frank listens more than he talks. North side men who know the attacker's family. The social geometry of what it would cost Frank to pursue this.
- Clara Mae's neighborhood — south side. Player can find her. She isn't surprised Frank hasn't done anything. She explains this without bitterness, which is worse.
- Ray Coldwater — Frank has been to the south side twice. Ray has noticed. They circle each other.
- The chief's office — player can be present for a conversation between Frank and his chief. The chief is not a monster. He's a man protecting the town as he understands it.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Let it rest. The chief's way. File goes cold. Frank carries it.
- **Progressive:** Pursue it formally. File a report over the chief's objection. Frank knows what this costs him.
- **Middle:** Build a case quietly. Get Ray to take a parallel complaint to the county level. Works the system from two angles simultaneously. Requires Frank to trust Ray enough to involve him — a significant ask in 1955.

**What's already underneath Frank in 1955:**

He's spent twenty years enforcing a law that doesn't apply evenly and telling himself the unevenness is temporary, a condition of the era, something that will correct itself. Clara Mae's file is the first time he's written down what he actually thinks in official language. The act of writing it changed something.

---

## 1967 — "The Man The Law Is Failing"

Frank is 52. He's made chief's detective by now — nominally a promotion, practically a leash. More visibility, less freedom. The chief is the same man, older, more entrenched.

Vietnam is happening. Frank's youngest son got a deferment through his college enrollment. Frank is aware of exactly what the south side's draft numbers look like. He has approximately the same list Ray has. He came to it through arrest records and injury reports. It's in his desk drawer.

His 1967 problem: Bobby Simmons shipped out. Frank knew Bobby — used to see him at the diner. Frank also knows the factory owner's son who got the deferment. They're in the same Rotary Club. Frank sat next to him last Tuesday.

**Conversation thread:**
- The Rotary Club meeting — Frank next to the factory owner's son. The player can be present. Frank's expression is controlled.
- Bobby's mother — she comes to the police station about something unrelated. Frank helps her. Brief scene. She mentions Bobby shipped out. Frank says the right thing. He means it. It's still the wrong amount.
- Ray's list — Ray gives it to Frank (if middle path chosen in 1955). Frank sits with it. The conversation they have is the first real one.
- Chambers' march — Frank is assigned to monitor it. Not to stop it. Just to be present. He watches Chambers walk. He watches Carver's window.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** The list stays in the drawer. Frank enforces the law as it's applied, not as it should be. He's not giving up — he's waiting for a better moment that may not come.
- **Progressive:** Frank takes the draft inequity to the county DA. It goes nowhere officially. But it's on record. Frank's relationship with his chief doesn't recover.
- **Middle:** Frank facilitates Marcus's reassignment quietly (the draft lottery middle path). Uses connections he's not supposed to have. It costs him something he can't itemize. He doesn't tell Ray what it cost.

---

## 1979 — "The Man Spending His Credit"

Frank is 64. He's been called in to investigate the Blanton accident. Three dead. He knows this investigation. He knows how it ends if left to run its normal course. He also has Geri's injury data (if that connection was built) and Ray's knowledge of the verbal complaint.

He's been building credit with both sides of Middletown for thirty years — careful, strategic, never spending more than he had. The Blanton investigation is going to cost him something significant regardless of what he does.

His personal complication: Harold Blanton Jr. is not a stranger. Their sons played baseball together. Frank has had dinner at the Blanton house. This is not relevant to the investigation. It is completely relevant to the investigation.

**Conversation thread:**
- The factory — Frank at the accident site. Player can accompany him. He's methodical. He doesn't perform emotion but something in how he moves through the space tells you he's carrying it.
- Geri's office — the circling conversation. Neither shows cards first. This is the scene where their relationship either deepens or stays professional.
- Ray and the Runningwater widow — Frank finds out about the toolbox. Not from Ray. From someone else. He doesn't tell Ray how he knows.
- Harold Blanton Jr. — Frank interviews him officially. The scene is different if Frank has eaten dinner at his house.
- The chief — new chief now, younger, political. Wants a clean resolution. Frank knows what clean means.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Frank builds the case he can build — negligence, not criminal. Harold faces civil liability, not prison. The families get settlements. Frank tells himself justice was approximate.
- **Progressive:** Frank pursues criminal charges. Uses everything Geri and Ray gave him. Harold is prosecuted. Frank's career doesn't survive the political fallout.
- **Middle:** Frank negotiates the outcome — Harold pleads to a lesser charge, factory stays open under real safety oversight, families get compensation that means something. Requires both Geri and Ray to have given him what they have. Requires the chief to believe Frank can manage the outcome. Enormous amount of accumulated trust spent in one moment.

---

## 1991 — "The Man With New Problems"

Frank is 76. Retired from the department in 1985. He consults occasionally. He still knows everyone and everyone still calls him.

The casino brings new faces. Frank notices them the way he's noticed everything for fifty years — cataloging, filing, not reacting until he understands the pattern. The pattern becomes clear by mid-1991. Drugs moving through new channels. Prostitution operations that didn't exist six months ago. Trafficking routes that use the casino's legitimate traffic as cover.

He has photographs. He's been taking them privately for three months before he decides to show Ray.

**The Frank and Ray scene:**

Frank brings Ray to the photographs. Shows him without editorializing. The violence in the photographs is casual. Practiced.

*"Ray. I been a cop in this town for thirty-six years. You know what I learned? Power doesn't redeem people. It just gives them new ways to disappoint you."*

Beat.

*"I'm not talking about your people. I'm talking about all of them. Mine included."*

This is the moment Ray decides whether to trust Frank.

**Conversation thread:**
- The crime scenes — Frank shows the player too. Different details depending on player character.
- The tribal council meeting — Frank attends as a private citizen. He watches who is and isn't surprised by what he's about to show Ray.
- Ray's godson — Frank has seen the godson's name in connection with the management company. He doesn't tell Ray this before showing him the photographs. He tells him after.

**The nudge (casino/rail reroute):**
- **Conservative:** Frank takes what he has to the state police. Bypasses local politics entirely. The casino deal is jeopardized. Ray doesn't speak to him for six months.
- **Progressive:** Frank gives everything to the tribal council's internal affairs process. Sovereignty argument. He's not sure he believes it but he respects it. The outcome is uncertain.
- **Middle:** Frank and Ray negotiate a jurisdictional agreement on the access roads as a condition of the rail reroute approval. Frank gets law enforcement presence. Ray gets his people's sovereignty respected. Neither gets everything. Both can live with it.

---

## 2008 — "The Man Who Kept The Books"

Frank is 93. Impossibly old. Still sharp. His body has opinions about this but his mind hasn't caught up with them.

He's the other living archive alongside Carver. Between the two of them they have a complete record of Middletown from 1935 forward — Carver from the pulpit, Frank from the street. They've been having lunch every other Tuesday for twenty years. Nobody knows this. It never seemed worth announcing.

The financial crisis. The tracks question. Frank has seen every version of this negotiation. He knows what the tribal council wants and what Cornerstone wants and what the railroad commission will accept. He's been the person in the room for thirty years where nobody was supposed to be taking notes.

He was taking notes.

**Arc endpoints:**
- **Conservative path:** Frank is the man who kept the institution honest when it could be kept honest and documented when it couldn't. The records exist. History will find them or it won't. He did his job.
- **Progressive path:** Frank is the man who spent his credit at the right moments. The Blanton case. The draft list. The casino jurisdictional agreement. He doesn't have credit left but the town has things it wouldn't have without him.
- **Middle path:** Frank is the man who kept both sides talking when both sides wanted to stop. Not because he was diplomatic — because he was inconvenient. Too informed to be dismissed by either faction.

**Final scene:**

Frank's kitchen. Tuesday lunch with Carver. The player is present — invited or stumbled upon, depending on path.

Carver is talking about something from 1955. Frank is eating his soup.

The player realizes: these two men have been having this conversation, in some form, for decades. The town has changed entirely around them. They're still here.

Frank looks up. Sees the player watching.

*"You've been here a long time."*

Not surprised. Not alarmed. Just noting it.

*"Longer than most."*

He goes back to his soup.

*"Carver thinks you're an angel. I think you're something else."*

He doesn't say what.

# JUNE HOLLOWAY (Politician)
**North side. City Council. Age 34 in 1955.**
*Named after Bruce's 5th grade teacher.*

---

## 1955 — "The Woman Elected To Agree"

June is 34. First woman on Middletown's city council. Elected on "progress" — a word both sides of every argument in Middletown claimed as their own. Her family money is oil. Her father's derrick is visible on the horizon from anywhere in the east part of town.

She's smarter than most people in the room and has learned to be strategic about when that shows. She got elected by being relatable and will govern by being effective. She's not sure yet if those two things are compatible.

Her 1955 problem: Cornerstone is coming. The city council vote is in six weeks. June has to cast the deciding vote. She already knows which way she's leaning. She's not sure if she's leaning that way because she believes it or because her father does.

**Conversation thread:**
- City council chambers — June reviewing the Cornerstone proposal. Player can read over her shoulder, so to speak. The economic projections are real. So are the risks to Main Street.
- The Holloway oil derrick — key scene. June performing for her father when the player arrives. Her father is enthusiastic about Cornerstone — economic development, progress, his kind of progress. He thinks June's council seat is a charming hobby. June sees the player arrive and her expression shifts almost imperceptibly. Caught mid-performance.
- Earl's Diner — the Main Street merchants. Earl himself, the hardware store owner, the Woolworth's manager. They have real fear. They also have real limitations the Cornerstone projections don't account for.
- Geri — if the player connects them, Geri has economic disparity data that reframes the Cornerstone question entirely. It's not just about Main Street. It's about which side of the tracks gets the jobs.
- Ray Coldwater — quiet conversation. What does Cornerstone mean for south side employment? Different question than the one June has been asking.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Vote to welcome Cornerstone. Economic development. Her father's position. She's not wrong about the jobs.
- **Progressive:** Vote to block Cornerstone. Protect Main Street. She's not wrong about the community cost.
- **Middle:** Negotiate conditions before the vote — local sourcing requirements, local hiring percentages, restrictions on certain product categories that directly compete with specific Main Street businesses. Requires June to get two other council members on board. Requires the player to have talked to enough people to know what conditions matter to whom.

**What's already underneath June in 1955:**

She has her father's pragmatism and her mother's conscience and she's been trying to figure out which one to lead with her entire adult life. The council seat was supposed to resolve this. It has not resolved this.

---

## 1967 — "The Woman Progress Left Behind"

June is 46. She's been on the council for twelve years. She's won two re-elections. She's no longer the first woman — there are two others now, which she considers her most durable achievement.

The Cornerstone decision has aged into whatever it aged into. Main Street looks like what it looks like. June knows which version she voted for.

The Civil Rights Act is three years old. Middletown is formally integrated and practically isn't. June has been pushing incremental changes through the council — housing ordinances, school district boundary adjustments, procurement policies that open south side businesses to city contracts. The progress is real. The pace is not.

Her 1967 problem: There's a city council vote coming on whether to support federal funding for a south side community development project. The funding comes with oversight requirements that the conservative faction finds objectionable. June has the votes to pass it but one of her votes is a man who will only support it if June publicly backs his highway project — which will route a new road through the south side and displace forty families.

**Conversation thread:**
- City hall — the council member with the highway project. June managing him. Player watches her work.
- The forty families — player can visit the south side residential area and see exactly who would be displaced. Specific faces. Not abstractions.
- Ray Coldwater — he knows about the highway project. He knows about June's vote. He's watching.
- Geri — if connected, she has data on outcomes from similar displacement projects in other towns. The numbers are not ambiguous.
- The Holloway derrick — June goes there after a hard council meeting. It's quieter than it was in 1955. Her father is older. They talk around what they mean to say.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Take the vote. Highway project goes through. Community development funded. Forty families displaced for a net positive outcome. June tells herself this is how governing works.
- **Progressive:** Refuse the deal. Lose the vote. Community development project fails. She keeps her conscience and loses the outcome.
- **Middle:** Find a third way — reroute the highway to minimize displacement, cost the project enough that the council member's support becomes cheaper than his opposition. Requires June to have built enough relationships on the council over twelve years to have alternatives. Requires the player to have supported her coalition-building since 1955.

---

## 1979 — "The Woman Whose Foundation Is Cracking"

June is 58. The oil bust is here. The Holloway family is not ruined but they are no longer comfortable in the way they were comfortable, which is its own kind of ruin for people who have never known anything else.

Her father's derrick is quiet. He hasn't said anything about it directly. Neither has she. The performance at the derrick in 1955 — whatever the player witnessed — has calcified into a permanent mutual pretense.

She's still on the council. Still effective. But the oil bust has exposed something about the conservative faction's economic philosophy that June has been quietly avoiding: it works in a boom and it doesn't have a plan for a bust.

The Blanton accident. Three dead. June votes on the city's response to the investigation. Whatever that looks like depends on what Frank and Geri and Ray have built.

Her 1979 problem: The city's budget is short because of the oil bust's effect on tax revenues. Something has to be cut. The conservative faction wants to cut south side services. The progressive faction wants to cut the highway maintenance budget (which serves mostly north side commuters). June holds the deciding vote again.

**Conversation thread:**
- Her father's derrick — standing there looking at the quiet pump jack. This is the scene where the performance finally cracks slightly. She doesn't cry. She says something practical. The player hears what's underneath the practical thing.
- City council chambers — the budget meeting. June managing two factions who are angrier than usual because everyone is scared.
- Earl's Diner — Dot has opinions about what gets cut. She's been watching this town for thirty years from behind that counter. June stops in. They have a real conversation for the first time.
- Geri — if connected, Geri has data on the long-term cost of cutting south side services versus the short-term savings. The numbers are uncomfortable for everyone.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Cut south side services. Protect the tax base. The logic is defensible. June knows it is and knows what it costs.
- **Progressive:** Cut highway maintenance. It's a north side burden and the north side can absorb it better. Loses the conservative faction permanently.
- **Middle:** Across-the-board proportional cuts with a protection floor for the three south side services with the highest health impact (clinic, school nutrition, sanitation). Requires Geri's data to know which three. Requires June to have built south side relationships over twenty years to know which cuts would be absorbed versus devastating.

---

## 1991 — "The Woman Who Became The Institution"

June is 70. She's been on the city council for 36 years with one two-year gap when she lost a re-election in 1973 (oil was still good, her opponent promised more of the same, she was asking harder questions). She came back. She's been asking harder questions ever since.

The casino/rail reroute question lands on her desk. The tribal council wants city support for the federal railroad commission process. The desegregation condition (retiring North/South designations) requires a city council vote. The Cornerstone question, whatever it became, shapes how the commercial community responds.

The north/south designation vote is the most significant thing June has voted on since Cornerstone. Everything her family's name is attached to is on the north side. She is going to vote to retire the designation. She's known this for thirty years. She's been waiting for someone to put it on the ballot.

**Conversation thread:**
- City council — the designation vote debate. June managing it. She's done this before. She knows which arguments are genuine and which are cover.
- The Holloway derrick — rusted, silent. Her father died in 1984. She goes there occasionally. Player can find her there. She doesn't explain why.
- Ray Coldwater — their first real peer conversation. Thirty-six years of parallel work in the same town.
- The post office — the Art Deco post office that will have to reissue addresses if the designation retires. The postmaster has opinions. June listens carefully.
- Frank — his jurisdictional agreement condition for the rail reroute requires city sign-off. June is the sign-off.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Support the casino deal but oppose the designation retirement — separate the economic question from the symbolic one. Keeps conservative allies. Loses Ray's coalition.
- **Progressive:** Full support — casino deal, designation retirement, post office reissue, the whole package as presented. Burns the last of her conservative relationships.
- **Middle:** Support with phasing — designation retirement approved but implementation over 18 months with a community process for renaming. Gives the post office time. Gives the north side a face-saving transition. Gives Ray a timetable he can hold the city to.

---

## 2008 — "The Woman Who Built Something"

June is 87. She retired from the council in 1999 — finally, genuinely retired, not bumped out. The woman who succeeded her was someone June had mentored for twelve years. She considers this the correct ending to her council career.

The financial crisis. Blanton's bailout. The tracks question. June's name is on enough history in this town that both sides still call her. She answers.

She's also dealing with something personal: the Holloway family land — the derrick site, the surrounding acreage — is being evaluated as a potential development site. The tribal corporation has expressed interest. The choice of what happens to her family's land is the last significant decision June Holloway will make.

**Arc endpoints:**
- **Conservative path:** June built a more efficient version of the town that existed in 1955. More functional, cleaner governance, some genuine progress. The tracks are still there in spirit even if they've moved in body. She gave Middletown better management. She's not sure that was enough.
- **Progressive path:** June burned her family's inheritance — social, financial, political — on a series of votes that changed the south side's institutional standing permanently. Her name means something different on each side of where the tracks used to be. She considers this correct.
- **Middle path:** June built the scaffolding that let Middletown keep arguing. Every deal, every compromise, every frustrating half-measure — the town is still standing and still fighting because the fight was never shut down by a clean victory for either side. She finds this exhausting and essential.

**Final scene:**

June at the Art Deco post office. She's been here dozens of times across sixty years of council business. The postmaster's desk has changed several times. The building hasn't.

She's holding a letter — official stationery, city address. Her old address. North Middletown.

The new address has no directional. Just a street name.

She looks at it for a long moment.

*"My father would have hated this."*

She puts it in her pocket.

*"He would have been wrong."*

She walks out. The clock on City Hall is visible through the post office window. Still running.

# DR. SAMUEL BEAUMONT (Medical Doctor)
**South side. Private Practice. Age 36 in 1955.**

---

## 1955 — "The Man Who Came Home To A Closed Door"

Samuel is 36. He grew up on the south side of Middletown's tracks, the son of a barber and a schoolteacher. He was the first from his family, and one of the first from the south side, to attend medical school. He trained in Chicago, where a Negro physician could get hospital privileges and practice without asking permission for every decision.

He came back. His mother was sick. She recovered. He stayed.

The north side hospital won't grant him privileges. The reasons given are procedural, rotational, bureaucratic — never the real reason. Samuel has filed three formal requests and received three procedural denials. He's stopped filing.

He practices out of the front room of his house. A hand-painted sign on the gate. A waiting room that is his living room with the good furniture pushed against the wall. He is the only doctor most of the south side has ever seen.

His 1955 problem: He is treating a patient — James Whitehorse, father of three, south side — whose drinking has a medical cause that nobody has diagnosed because nobody with hospital access has examined him. The right diagnosis requires equipment Samuel doesn't have and a specialist referral the north side hospital won't process for a south side patient from an unlicensed practitioner.

**Conversation thread:**
- Samuel's practice — waiting room when the player arrives. People waiting with the patience of people who have learned that waiting is the condition. Samuel emerges from the back. He's been on his feet since 5am. He sees the player the way he sees patients — assessing, filing, moving on.
- The north side hospital — player can visit. The admissions desk. The procedural distance. The particular politeness of an institution that has decided what it is.
- The IHS clinic — Samuel goes there weekly. Ray is sometimes there. They've been working in parallel for three years without formally acknowledging the collaboration.
- Geri — if the player connects them, Geri has the tuberculosis data that Samuel has been seeing in his practice without the statistical framework. The meeting between them is the meeting of two people who have been looking at the same thing from different angles.
- Marcus Beaumont — Samuel's nephew, 14, works the crossing signal after school. Samuel is raising him informally after Marcus's father died. He doesn't say this to anyone. It's just what he does.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Work within the system — continue filing requests, build a case through official channels, protect his medical license by staying procedurally correct.
- **Progressive:** Go around the system — build a relationship with a sympathetic north side physician who can technically process the referrals under his license while Samuel does the actual medicine. Gray area. If discovered, both careers are at risk.
- **Middle:** Go to Ray. The IHS clinic has a relationship with a federal Indian Health Service hospital in the state that processes referrals independently of the north side hospital. Technically it's for tribal members. Ray can navigate the paperwork. Samuel gets James Whitehorse seen by a specialist. It costs Ray a favor and Samuel a principle about which rules he's willing to bend.

**What's already underneath Samuel in 1955:**

He has the controlled precision of a man who was told in seventeen different ways in medical school that he wasn't supposed to be there. He passed every examination, treated every patient, published a paper in his third year, and smiled when people expressed surprise that he spoke so well.

He came home because Middletown needed a doctor. He is furious about what Middletown is doing with that gift. He does not show this. He shows up at 5am and sees patients until they stop coming.

---

## 1967 — "The Man The System Is Starting To Tolerate"

Samuel is 48. The Civil Rights Act is three years old. The north side hospital has granted him provisional privileges — admitting rights but no surgical suite access, no department head eligibility, no on-call rotation. Provisional. The word is doing a lot of work.

He has moved his practice to a small office building on the edge of the south side — still south side, but a step removed from his living room. He has one nurse. He's treating three times as many patients as he was in 1955.

Marcus got his draft notice.

Samuel is the one who tells Marcus's mother. He sits with her for two hours. He does not tell her what he knows about the draft inequity statistics. He just sits with her.

His 1967 problem: A young Black man from his practice — not Marcus, another patient, 19 years old, name of Darnell — has been arrested for a crime Samuel doesn't believe he committed. The evidence is circumstantial. The arresting officer is a man Samuel has had procedural dealings with. Frank DeLuca is not the arresting officer. Frank knows about the arrest.

**Conversation thread:**
- Samuel's office — he tells the player about Darnell directly. Not asking for help. Stating facts. Watching what the player does with them.
- The police station — Frank's desk. The Darnell file. Frank has questions about the arrest that he's asking in a way that suggests he already knows the answers.
- Ray — has the county resources to provide Darnell's family with legal support. Has used them before. Is tired of using them.
- The provisional hospital privileges meeting — player can be present. The hospital board. The particular language of almost-acceptance.
- Geri's report — if she's presenting her integration findings this period, Samuel's health outcome data is the corroborating voice. Their joint presentation, if the player made it possible, is the scene where two people who've been working in parallel finally work together visibly.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Samuel focuses on what he can control — Darnell's medical welfare, documented testimony as Darnell's physician. Stays in his lane.
- **Progressive:** Samuel goes to the press with what he knows. Uses his medical authority to give credibility to a story that otherwise gets buried. His provisional hospital privileges become un-provisional immediately.
- **Middle:** Samuel gives Frank everything he has as Darnell's physician, framed as a medical statement, not a political one. Gives Frank the tool. Lets Frank decide how to use it. Requires Frank to be someone Samuel has reason to trust.

---

## 1979 — "The Man The Hospital Still Won't Fully Open"

Samuel is 60. His provisional privileges have become full privileges — for everything except the surgical suite and department head eligibility. The language has changed. The ceiling has not.

The Blanton accident. Two of the three dead are his patients. He performs the autopsies. The cause of death is not complicated. The manner — what led to the cause — is documented in his reports with the same precision he brings to everything.

His 1979 problem: He was treating one of the dead workers, a man named George Runningwater, for a respiratory condition that Samuel believed was occupational in origin. He documented this. He filed a report with the county health department eight months ago. The report is in a drawer somewhere.

This is different from Geri's predictive analysis and Ray's verbal complaint. This is medical documentation in official channels. It was filed and ignored.

**Conversation thread:**
- The morgue at the north side hospital — Samuel doing the autopsies. The player can be present. He works in silence. When he finishes he sits for a moment before cleaning up. Just a moment.
- Frank — Samuel gives Frank the respiratory documentation voluntarily, before Frank asks. He's been waiting for someone to ask.
- George Runningwater's wife — she's at the IHS clinic. Samuel sees her there. They don't talk about the accident. He checks her blood pressure. Gives her something for the headaches she's been having. She thanks him.
- The hospital board — a meeting about safety protocols in the wake of the accident. Samuel is present as a treating physician. He is not asked to speak. He speaks anyway.
- IHS clinic — the federal health service is starting to fund clinic improvements. Small but real. Samuel has been advocating for this for twelve years.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Samuel's medical documentation enters the investigation through Frank. He testifies if called. He stays in his medical role.
- **Progressive:** Samuel presents his findings publicly — medical conference, press, wherever people will listen. His documentation becomes the moral center of the accountability argument.
- **Middle:** Samuel submits his documentation to the state medical board as a professional standards matter — separate track from Frank's criminal investigation, harder to bury, slower to resolve, ultimately more durable.

---

## 1991 — "The Man With A Choice"

Samuel is 72. He should be slowing down. His waiting room is still full at 7am.

The tribal hospital is under construction. The IHS clinic is being replaced by a facility that will have more equipment than the north side hospital. The tribal council's project director has come to Samuel directly: will he join the new hospital's staff? Full privileges. Department co-chair eligibility. His own surgical suite if he wants it.

Everything the north side hospital denied him for thirty-six years, offered by the south side.

His 1991 problem: He wants to say yes. He also has north side patients who have been coming to him for thirty years, who have no other doctor they trust, and who cannot easily access a tribal facility. He also has staff at the north side hospital — younger physicians, one of them a Black woman who got her privileges partly because of how Samuel positioned himself over thirty-six years — who would read his departure as abandonment.

**Conversation thread:**
- The tribal hospital construction site — Samuel walking through the building in progress. The project director showing him where things will be. Samuel touching walls the way doctors touch patients.
- His north side patients — Earl is one. An older man who has been coming to Samuel since 1962. Earl never treated Samuel well in 1955. He's been a decent patient for thirty years. He has a heart condition that requires continuity of care.
- The north side hospital's chief of medicine — younger, genuinely different from the men who denied Samuel in 1955. Comes to Samuel privately. Asks him to stay.
- Marcus — back from Vietnam, married, two kids. Working at Blanton. He doesn't tell Samuel what he went through. Samuel doesn't ask. They talk about the kids' health.
- Ray — they've been colleagues in the complicated sense for thirty-six years. Ray has an opinion about the tribal hospital offer that he delivers in about eleven words.

**The nudge:**
- **Conservative:** Stay at his current practice. The north side patients. The younger physicians at the hospital who need his example there. Decline the tribal offer respectfully.
- **Progressive:** Accept the tribal offer. Full move. His practice closes, north side patients referred to other physicians. He's earned this.
- **Middle:** Split appointment — tribal hospital privileges and department co-chair, but maintaining his south side private practice as a bridge clinic that serves both populations. Exhausting. Possibly impossible. He's 72. He does it anyway.

---

## 2008 — "The Man Who Stayed Open"

Samuel is 89. He is not practicing medicine anymore — stopped at 85 when his hands told him to. He is, however, at the tribal hospital most days. Consulting. Teaching. Sitting in the break room talking to residents who don't yet know they should be listening more carefully.

The tribal hospital is the best medical facility within 60 miles. This is not a coincidence. Samuel knows the names of every physician on staff and the specific obstacles each one had to navigate to get there.

The financial crisis. The Blanton bailout. The tracks.

Samuel's arc in 2008 is quieter than the others. He has done his work. The institutions exist or they don't based on fifty years of decisions. He watches the 2008 arguments the way a man watches a river he helped dig the channel for.

His Marcus: Marcus Beaumont is 67. Samuel's nephew, the boy at the crossing, the soldier who came back. He's been on the tribal council for twelve years. He's one of the people in the room for the Blanton bailout negotiation. Samuel watches this and doesn't say what he thinks because Marcus doesn't need him to anymore.

**Arc endpoints:**
- **Conservative path:** Samuel built a practice and then a bridge and then a reputation. The north side hospital still exists. The tribal hospital exists beside it. Neither swallowed the other. He made peace with the ceiling above him and built a different building.
- **Progressive path:** Samuel's move to the tribal hospital became the statement that changed the north side hospital's self-understanding. They lost him. They changed because they lost him. He wasn't trying to change them. It happened anyway.
- **Middle path:** Samuel built the bridge and kept walking back and forth across it for thirty-five years until the bridge became the town.

**Final scene:**

Samuel is in the tribal hospital break room. Early morning. He has a cup of coffee. A young resident — first year, overwhelmed, not showing it as well as she thinks — sits down across from him.

She doesn't know who he is. Not fully. She knows he consults here. She's been on shift for eighteen hours.

He slides his coffee toward her.

She starts to decline. He's already stood up.

*"I've been on my feet since 1955,"* he says. *"I know what tired looks like."*

He puts on his coat.

*"Drink the coffee. The patients need you more than they need me today."*

He pauses at the door.

*"They always did."*

He leaves. She drinks the coffee. Through the break room window the tribal hospital parking lot is full at 6am. Both sides of what used to be the tracks.
